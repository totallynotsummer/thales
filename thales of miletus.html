Thales of Miletus




Thales:

Thales of Miletus was one of the legendary Seven Wise Men, who contributed a lot into bridging the worlds of myth and reason. He was born approximately in 624-620 BCE in Miletus and died in 548-545 BCE at 78 years old, which meant he belonged to the Pre-Socratic Era. He was the first Greek philosopher and was the first to give a purely natural explanation on the origin of the world that did not include any mythology. Since Thales was born such a long time ago, we have no information about his life except for a few roughly established dates on when he made his discoveries.

Thales may have learnt the fundamentals of geometry in Egypt, but there is proof that the Egyptians excelled in fields that were not geometry. Therefore, Thales identified and noticed the features of lines, angles and circles and understood particular principles which he had checked through continuous demonstration. He was the first to state that the angle on the inside of a semicircle will always be a right angle. Aristotle was very interested in this fact and has asked a question in one of his works:” Why is the angle in a semicircle always a right angle?” Aristotle did not answer this question, nor did he mention it in any way after asking the question.

Thales believed the world was a sphere and there are many good reasons for that. One, during a solar eclipse, the fact that the shadow is caused by the position of the Earth between the sun and moon. Since the moon is always curved outwards, the Earth must be spherical. Two, Thales was known to observe the stars so he would have noticed that stars which are visible in a certain place cannot be seen further north or south which leads to the conclusion that the Earth is round. Three, when we look up and see the sky, it feels like we are inside a dome and the sky seems to surround the Earth.
